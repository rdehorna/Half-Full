Persistent Depressive Disorder (dysthymia)

Definition by American Psychiatric Association DSM-5:

  1. Depressed mood present for most of the day and close to every day for at least 2 years
  2. The depressed mood with 2 or more of the following symptoms:
    • Decreased or increased appetite
    • Insomnia or hypersomnia
    • Low energy
    • Poor self-esteem
    • Poor concentration
    • Hopelessness
  3. During the 2 years, the individual has not gone without symptoms for more than 2 months at a time
  4. Does not meet the criteria, for manic or hypomanic episodes or cyclothymic disorder
  5. Symptoms are not explained by other effects such as drugs, other health conditions, or medications
  6. Symptoms may cause distress in social and occupational environments

Risk Factors for Major Depressive Disorder The risk factors for depression involve genetic, medical health, environmental and social influences:

  • Prior depressive episode
  • Family history
  • Female gender
  • Childhood trauma
  • Stressful life events
  • Poor social support
  • Serious medical illness
  • Dementia
  • Substance abuse

Treatment

Depression can be successfully treated and treatment is more effective when depression is treated earlier. Talk to your healthcare provider about all your symptoms to determine the correct type of depression and treatment depending on severity. The treatment options includes psychotherapy or “talk therapy” and antidepressant medication. Talk therapy allows the individual to make a choice to involve a range of people from just the individual, family, friends, and group therapy. Half Full does not replace talk therapy but integrates into the treatment through the use of technology.

Importance of monitoring

Many of the antidepressant medications take time to work and therefore monitoring plays an important during this time. With the treatment for depression, monitoring with Half Full allows you to understand what is treatments are working for you when progress is sometimes difficult to see.

Depression is common and frequently undetected. Detecting depression is not enough to improve the clinical outcome. Clinical outcomes can be improved by seeking treatment and careful monitoring by the patient and help from Half Full.
